CensoredBooks

An information retrieval system that finds banned books in schools and libraries across the US.

As a team of five, our goal was to develop an information retrieval systems for the topic of our choosing. We decided to focus on banned books since censorship is a major topic of debate in the US.

In order to create the IR system, we needed data. I developed a web scraping solution that extracted book information from GoodReads based on a banned book list from PEN America. By collecting and cleaning the data, we had the foundation of our IR system.

We developed the mechanics of our IR system using Python. We have two main searches: Title and Theme. The Title search uses Boolean index searching and minimum edit distance algorithms to find banned books by their title. The Theme search uses singular value decomposition (SVD) based on the Users (book reviews) and cosine similarity based on the Publishers (book summaries). We also developed filters for searching for a specific state or genre.

We held multiple in-person tech demos of our product, explaining technical concepts to over 100 colleagues. Our team presented our product at BOOM 24, Cornell's premier showcase for student-developed technology. We designed a poster capturing book covers and displaying visualization to raise awareness on censorship. We earned an Honorable Mention from the Information Science Department!

Race and Higher Education Selectivity

An analysis on the relationship between race and college selectivity.

As a team of three, our goal was to analyze how a specific domain and sensitive features would be affected by machine learning algorithms. We focused on higher education and performed literature reviews on related academic papers. We researched how the racial makeup of a college could indicate its selectivity.

We collected and cleaned the data using Python and conducted explorations of the data. We mainly focused on the college representation by racial group. In this case, college representation is the percentage of individuals of a certain racial group minus the percentage of geographically local individuals of the racial group.

We developed 3 logistic regression models using a train/test split to see how race as an input predicts the selectivity of a college (our outcome variable): No Race, POC Lumped (percentages of people of color combined together), and POC Granular (percentages of people of color listed separately).

We determined that the POC Granular model performed the best for more selective colleges, with an accuracy of 0.810 and an AUC score of 0.829. Our POC Granular model also performs the best for non selective colleges. However, our POC Granular model underperforms on selective colleges compared to the other models.

QuadLink

A two-player game inspired by Connect 4 with 5 unique game modes: Classic, Gravity Shift, Snap Crackle Pop, That's A Wrap, and Custom.

As a team of three, we created a game by utilizing the functional programming language OCaml. We created modules to store our game mechanics and board, using linear algebra to implement some of the more unique game modes. We performed gameplay testing with random students to refine our game. We achieved 99% code coverage and maintained detailed documentation in the code.

The game mode Classic allows you to play a typical game of Connect 4. The mode Gravity Shift allows a player to move the location of all tokens to the direction they choose. The mode Snap Crackle Pop allows a player to remove any piece on the board. The mode That's A Wrap causes the board to wrap around every few turns. The mode Custom allows players to play Classic on a board size of their choosing.
